[1] Many public schools cut music and art programs when faced with budget constraints such as resulted from California's 1988 Proposition 13 that slashed property taxes. [2] The Clinton administration emphasized improving arts education and some local governments increased school art and music budgets. [3] The entertainment industry lobbied to get arts education back into schools and philanthropic organizations have donated funds. [4] Strategies for increasing art and music education include magnet schools and teaching teachers to blend arts into core subjects. [5] Community art resources often expand school programs. [6] LA schools added visual or performing arts to graduation requirements and California's public university system required them for admission. [7] Digital technology and interactive learning centers have increasingly been used to interest children in the arts, through private or cultural institutions or on CD-ROM and the Web. [8] Advocates for school art and music classes claim music is linked to IQ. [9] Music programs help Hispanic students learn English more quickly. [10] But measuring gains from arts education is difficult and finding resources remains problematic. [11] In 2000, still only 25% of US schools included regular music programs. [12] Teachers object that arts programs occupy too much time and parents need to be convinced that arts are as important as math, reading and science. [13] Toughened academic standards cause arts electives to get dropped to make room for drill time to pass state exams. [14] Worldwide, China is attempting to improve arts education by providing free concerts, traditional operas and plays for students, and training art and music teachers for rural areas.
