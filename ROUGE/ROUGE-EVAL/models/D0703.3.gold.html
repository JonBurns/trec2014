[1] Prior to the scheduled introduction of the euro, polls indicated most EU citizens were in favor and believe it will be successful. [2] However, only 20% felt well informed about upcoming changes. [3] Despite media skepticism, the European Commission maintained it would be introduced on schedule January 1, 1999, initially for cashless trading. [4] The founding euro countries were Belgium, Germany, Spain, France, Ireland, Italy, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, Austria, Portugal and Finland. [5] Euro design development began in 1996. [6] That year, Britain's mortgage lenders prepared for euro mortgages. [7] In 1997, the code EUR was ascribed to the euro; Zambia expected to benefit from the currency; and Germany's Bundesbank claimed investors would benefit. [8] Italy was to make provisions for Vatican City and San Marino, which used Italian lire, to use euros. [9] France was to do the same for Monaco, St. Pierre and Miquelon, which used French francs. [10] Signs of the euro's acceptance included: two international banks quoting prices in the euro; China welcoming its initiation; Eurostat offering euro-related information online; Bulgaria moving to link its currency with the euro; and Thailand considering using it in foreign reserves. [11] However, the European Central Bank announced it wouldn't encourage it as reserve currency. [12] By late 1998, Romania's Central Bank included the euro in hard currency exchange rates and performed euro services. [13] The Reserve Bank of India permitted Indian banks to do euro transactions. [14] France, Finland, Belgium and Spain had begun production of euros. [15] Germany was preparing to do so. [16] Euros would become legal tender on January 1, 2002.
