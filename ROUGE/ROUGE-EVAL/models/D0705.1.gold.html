[1] In June 1996 the Basque separatist group ETA declared a week-long cease-fire in its 27-year violent struggle for independence. [2] ETA demanded that the Spanish government change its policy of placing ETA prisoners in jails far outside Basque country. [3] The government called on ETA to give up its arms altogether. [4] Nothing came of the cease-fire. [5] Violence by the ETA continued through 1997 and into 1998. [6] In June 1998 the political ally of ETA, Herri Batasuma (HB) demanded a negotiated solution to violence calling for Basque independence and improved conditions for ETA prisoners. [7] The Spanish Prime Minister ruled out negotiation with HB unless it distanced itself from ETA or ETA laid down its arms. [8] In September a coalition of Basque nationalists including HB signed the Lizarra Declaration calling on ETA to renounce violence. [9] ETA declared an open-ended cease-fire. [10] In November the Spanish government agreed to preliminary talks with ETA but would only negotiate terms for ETA to surrender its weapons and the fate of more than 500 ETA prisoners. [11] It offered to transfer some prisoners to Basque country if ETA renounced violence for good. [12] An ETA spokesman said transfer of all prisoners would be a step towards peace talks. [13] Spain offered to move 21 prisoners closer to Basque country but insisted on an end to violence. [14] A single round of talks ended in stalemate and the truce ended in December 1999. [15] By September 2000 twelve more ETA-related killings were recorded and French authorities had arrested the Chief of ETA.
