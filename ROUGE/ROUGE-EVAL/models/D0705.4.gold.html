[1] The Basque separatist group ETA conducted a weeklong unilateral ceasefire in Spain in June 1996. [2] The truce ended in July with a series of bomb attacks on tourist resorts. [3] In October 1997, ETA was preparing for another ceasefire when all 23 leaders of its political wing were jailed for distributing a pro-ETA video. [4] In the first half of 1998 six slayings were attributed to ETA. [5] Thousands demonstrated against the violence in the Basque region. [6] The Socialist Party withdrew from the three-party Basque regional government. [7] In August ETA said for the first time that it was opposed to street violence as a means of furthering the Basque cause. [8] Basque nationalists joined with other political groups to urge ETA to seek a permanent end to the violence and in September ETA announced an open-ended ceasefire. [9] The Spanish government agreed to hold peace talks, but said that there would be no discussion of Basque independence. [10] There was some discussion of prisoner transfers and some inmates were moved closer to the Basque region. [11] In November the current Spanish government and ETA made direct contact for the first time. [12] A hard crackdown soon followed, however, ending all contact and alienating Basque moderates. [13] The truce ended in December 1999, followed by an escalation in violence including at least 12 slayings in the ensuing eight months and the bombing of a Spanish disco. [14] ETA's Chief was arrested in France in August. [15] Demonstrations were held in September to protest the deaths of four Basque separatists.
