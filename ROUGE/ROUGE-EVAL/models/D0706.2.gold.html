[1] Since seizing power in 1988, Myanmar's military rulers have used forced labor, torture and rape to maintain power. [2] Junta leadership includes Senior General Than Shwe and General Tin Oo. [3] The opposition party NLD won 1990 elections, but the junta won't honor the results. [4] NLD leader and Nobel Peace laureate Suu Kyi has repeatedly demanded parliament be convened and political prisoners be released to no avail. [5] NLD vice chairman Tin Oo was threatened with arrest. [6] Britain pressured the regime to hold talks. [7] The US and EU placed economic sanctions on Myanmar to encourage negotiations. [8] Prior to the tenth anniversary of Myanmar's military takeover, about 500-900 political arrests were made, including members of the ABSDF, led by Ko Thein. [9] On the anniversary, exiled dissidents protested in Bangkok and Bangladesh. [10] The US and some European countries boycotted the 4th International Heroin Conference in Myanmar because of its drug trafficking and poor human rights record. [11] It's become the world's leading source of heroin and the epicenter of a regional AIDS crisis. [12] Myanmar's government reported over 500 anti-government activists surrendered in March 1999. [13] By then, 17 groups had laid down arms. [14] In early October, armed men peacefully seized the Myanmar Embassy in Bangkok, demanding release of political prisoners and recognition of the 1990 elections. [15] In 2000, Burmese guerrillas stormed a Thai hospital. [16] The US agreed to accept 1,500 Burmese student refugees from Thailand. [17] Myanmar was denied US anti-drug certification. [18] Suu Kyi was awarded the Dublin, Ireland Freedom of the City honor for her pro-democracy activism.
