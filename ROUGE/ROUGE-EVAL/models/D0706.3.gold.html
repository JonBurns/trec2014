[1] Myanmar has been ruled by the military in various guises since 1962. [2] After crushing a nationwide democracy movement, the State Law and Order Restoration Council took over Burma in 1998 and changed its name to Myanmar. [3] Nobel laureate Aung San Suu Kyi heads the popular opposition political party, the National League for Democracy. [4] Given her personal charisma and extraordinary family history, she has a strong claim on the allegiance of the Burmese people. [5] The military government has maintained a campaign to harass and imprison her and members of the NLD, anti-government ethnic armed groups, and student organizations. [6] Military bungling has turned resource-rich Myanmar to one of the world's poorest countries. [7] The poor economy is a source of widespread discontent. [8] The government has used forced labor and torture in its war against stubborn resistance by ethnic minorities. [9] Myanmar has demanded that the Thai government strictly control refugee camps on the Thai side of the border between the two countries. [10] A government official claimed these camps were used for military and terrorist training. [11] The government has continued to rebuff appeals for talks with the democratic opposition. [12] The regime has been accused of drug trafficking and widespread human rights violations. [13] The US and Europe boycotted an international heroin conference in Myanmar for these reasons. [14] Myanmar has continued to be a principal source heroin and has been identified as a source of the stimulant methamphetamine smuggled into Thailand. [15] It was denied US anti-drug certification in the 2000 release of its annual anti-drug certification report.
