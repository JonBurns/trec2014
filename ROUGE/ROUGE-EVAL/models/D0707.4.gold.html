[1] For many years Turkey sought to become a member of the European Union and in 1987 submitted a formal application. [2] The EU rejected it, causing relations to deteriorate. [3] The two formed a customs union and Turkey became the EU's largest trade partner. [4] Membership was not under consideration, however. [5] As of January 1998, relations between the two were bitter. [6] EU leaders had not included Turkey as a prospective member during its summit and in retaliation Turkey stopped its political dialogue with the Union. [7] In March Germany said that it did not envision Turkey as an EU member anytime soon. [8] In June Greece said that it supported Turkey's bid for membership. [9] In November the EU included Turkey as one of 12 candidate countries. [10] In August 1999 the EU sent 2 million euros in emergency aid after a massive earthquake hit Turkey and, after a second quake, was considering a second package. [11] In September Italy and Greece issued statements supporting Turkey's candidacy and in December the Helsinki summit agreed to have Turkey recognized as a candidate. [12] In 2000, in a move to ease European concerns about Turkey's human rights record, Turkey postponed the hanging of a Kurdish rebel leader until the European Court of Human Rights could hear his appeal. [13] In February the EU granted Turkey $750 million in loans. [14] In March a bilateral meeting focused on Turkey's economic stability program. [15] Three months later Turkey and other non-EU members were excluded from a European defense mechanism.
