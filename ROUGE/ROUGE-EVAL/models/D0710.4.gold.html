[1] Two Israelis, who claimed to be teachers, were arrested in November 1998 on suspicion of spying near a Cypriot military installation. [2] They had just telephoned an intelligence institute in Tel Aviv before being seized. [3] Police found listening and recording devices and other electronic equipment in their apartment. [4] A court ordered them detained pending investigation. [5] The Israeli press reported that the two men were Mossad agents and part of an intelligence operation. [6] Israel admitted that they were Mossad agents, but denied they were spying on behalf of Turkey or acting against Cyprus. [7] Following the arrests, Israel sent political and security officials to Cyprus to negotiate the suspects' release. [8] Mossad officials sent messages to Cyprus to dispel concerns that they were spying on behalf of Turkey or gathering information on Cypriot military installations. [9] The Israeli Prime Minister vowed to do everything he could to bring the men home. [10] The two suspects were brought before a remand hearing on where formal charges could be filed or the prosecution might ask for further remand. [11] They were formally charged and a trial was set for late December. [12] They pleaded guilty of approaching a prohibited military area and were sentenced to three years in prison. [13] After five months the Cyprus pardoned the two Israelis and suspended their prison terms, a goodwill gesture to the new Israeli Government. [14] In the wake of the arrests, one in a series of recent Mossad embarrassments, a senior Mossad operations official announced his retirement.
