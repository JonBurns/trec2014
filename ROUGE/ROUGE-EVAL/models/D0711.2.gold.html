[1] In a 1994 Justice Department settlement Microsoft corrected some illegal licensing practices with computer makers. [2] In 1995 Justice stopped Microsoft's buying Intuit. [3] In 1998, antitrust settlement talks delayed shipment of Windows 98. [4] Microsoft was allowed to integrate its internet browser into Windows. [5] The Justice Department and 19 states began antitrust trials against Microsoft. [6] In November 1998 a California court ordered Microsoft to stop violating its JAVA license with Sun Microsystems or stop shipping JAVA products. [7] Japan told Microsoft to permanently stop bundling EXCEL and WORD software to computer makers. [8] In 1999 the US antitrust suit claimed Microsoft illegally coerced IBM to "eliminate or drop" its OS/2 operating system else pay higher Windows prices. [9] In 1999 antitrust Judge Jackson issued "findings of fact" that Microsoft was a monopoly that abused its power. [10] Microsoft was still defending a case by Sun and Caldera. [11] In 2000 a third-party brief to Jackson foreshadowed a possible antitrust ruling. [12] Settlement talks collapsed just before Jackson issued his "findings of law". [13] In April 2000 Judge Jackson found that Microsoft violated the Sherman Antitrust Act, "through predatory and anticompetitive behavior", by keeping "an oppressive thumb on the scale of competitive fortune". [14] Microsoft said it would appeal. [15] Microsoft's aggressive browser marketing practices were judged not illegal. [16] In June 2000 Judge Jackson ordered Microsoft split into two separate companies: one for operating systems, another for other products, because "Microsoft has proved untrustworthy", citing Microsoft's failure to comply with a 1990's court ruling. [17] Microsoft expected to avoid breakup on appeal.
