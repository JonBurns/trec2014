[1] Antitrust action against Microsoft has been ongoing since 1990. [2] In 1994, the Justice Department reached a settlement with Microsoft requiring the company to change its business practices. [3] In 1995, Justice sued to block Microsoft's acquisition of Intuit. [4] In October 1996, The U.S. Government and 20 states launched a trial accusing Microsoft of illegal activities including bullying competitors. [5] Sun Microsystems sued Microsoft for violating their agreement related to the Java programming language and Caldera Linux sued Microsoft for its efforts to quash Dr. DOS, a competitor with Microsoft DOS. [6] In April 2000, Judge Thomas Penfield Jackson ruled that Microsoft violated the Sherman antitrust act. [7] He wrote that the company maintained its monopoly power by anti-competitive means, attempted to monopolize the web browser market and unlawfully tied its web browser to the operating system. [8] The Justice Department and 17 state attorneys general proposed breaking Microsoft up into two companies, one holding the operating system and the other everything else. [9] Judge Jackson agreed with the proposal. [10] The head of the Justice Department's anti-trust division said the Department is committed to finding a remedy that will protect consumers by putting an end to Microsoft's abuse of monopoly powers and rectify its unlawful attempt to monopolize the web browser market. [11] Bill Gates announced that his company will appeal the verdict which was characterized as an anti-capitalist attack that would be against the public interest and dangerous for the welfare of the economy.
