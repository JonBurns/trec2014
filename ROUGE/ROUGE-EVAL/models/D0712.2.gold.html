[1] In February 1989, late Iranian leader Ayatollah Khomeini issued a fatwa, pronouncing a death sentence against British author Salman Rushdie for his1988 novel The Satanic Verses. [2] Khomeini ruled the book blasphemous to Islam and exhorted all Moslems to execute the sentence. [3] For almost 10 years, Rushdie lived in safe houses around London, guarded around the clock. [4] He was persona non grata in his native India, the first country to ban his book. [5] The controversial problem hindered relations between Iran and European countries. [6] In 1996, the Iranian government began softening its stance on the edict, claiming it was a religious rather than executive decree, and that it never intended to carry it out. [7] Islamic hardliners and clerics in and out of Iran protested that the government didn't have the right to change a fatwa and called for Rushdie's death. [8] A private Iranian foundation raised its bounty on Rushdie from $2 million to $2.5, then $2.8 million. [9] A hard-line student group offered a 1 billion riyal ($333,000) reward. [10] Residents of an Iranian village offered land and carpets as bounty. [11] Thousands of Iranian clerics and theological students pledged a month's salary and said they were ready to execute the edict. [12] In September 1998, the Iranian government disassociated itself from the fatwa and any reward. [13] The EU welcomed the essential lifting of the death sentence. [14] A year later, Rushdie said his life had improved, though still not 100% normal. [15] Eleven years after its issue, Islamic hard-liners still claimed the fatwa to be in effect.
