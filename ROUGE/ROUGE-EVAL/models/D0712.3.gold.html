[1] In February, 1989, the late Iranian leader Ayatollah Khomeini issued a religious edict, a fatwa, pronouncing a death sentence on India-born, British novelist Salman Rushdie. [2] Rushdie's book "The Satanic Verses" is believed by Moslems to defame Islam and therefore mark him for death. [3] Seven years later, the Iranian Government, under pressure from European countries, dropped the death sentence. [4] President Rafsanjani said that it "was never meant to be carried out". [5] Eventually this led to the establishment of full diplomatic relations between Iran and the UK. [6] This announcement led Rushdie to believe that it constituted an end to the furtive life he had been leading under British police protection for several years. [7] He was finally able to appear in public and eventually was able to visit the land of his birth, India, where he had been unwelcome for several years. [8] Despite the Iranian Government's dropping of the death sentence, others in Iran believe that only Iran's Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei has the authority to cancel the death sentence. [9] Several groups, independent of the Iranian Government, began to offer bounties for the murder of Rushdie. [10] The Khordad Foundation offered $2.5M for killing Rushdie. [11] A student group at the Association of Hezbollah University offered over $300K to kill Rushdie. [12] Residents of a village in northern Iran offered carpets and land to anyone who would get rid of Rushdie. [13] Thousands of Iranian clerics and theological students have pledged a month's salary for his murder.
