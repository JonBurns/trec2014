[1] In March 1996, under pressure from European countries, the Iranian President made a controversial announcement canceling the death sentence on Salman Rushdie issued by the late Iranian leader Ayatollah Khomeini. [2] It had become a major problem hindering relations between Iran and the European countries In February 1997, a private Iranian foundation raised its reward for killing Rushdie from $2 million to $2.5 million. [3] The Iranian President declared that the government had no connection with this award and the foreign minister reiterated that statement at the UN in September. [4] The international reaction was mixed. [5] Britain restored diplomatic relations with Iran. [6] Pakistani Islamic groups were outraged. [7] The European Union welcomed the move saying that it would lead to closer cooperation. [8] India, having refused a visa for Rushdie, who was born in India, later said that he could visit India, but that his book would still be banned. [9] The controversy continued inside Iran. [10] In October 1998 more than half of the members of Iran's Parliament signed a letter saying that the death edict was still valid. [11] A hard-line Iranian student group offered 1 billion riyals ($333,333) for Rushdie's murder. [12] The private Iranian foundation raised its reward for killing Rushdie from $2.5 million to $2.8 million. [13] About 1000 people demonstrated outside Tehran University, shouting that the edict was still in force. [14] Thousands of Iranian clerics and theological students pledged a month's salary toward a bounty for killing Rushdie. [15] And villagers in north Iran offered land and carpets to anyone who killed Rushdie.
