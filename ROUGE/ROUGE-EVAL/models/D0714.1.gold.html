[1] Since its August 1999 debut, San Mateo, California-based Napster has accumulated over 10 million users and three lawsuits. [2] Claiming Napster's only product, a free file-sharing application, encourages copyright infringement, the Recording Industry Association of America sued, seeking $100,000 for each song illegally traded. [3] The heavy-metal band Metallica sued Napster and three universities for copyright infringement and racketeering, seeking $10 million in damages. [4] The universities were dropped from the suit after agreeing to block the site from their computer networks. [5] Rapper Dr. Dre also filed a similar lawsuit. [6] Metallica's drummer hand-delivered a 60,000-page list of over 335,000 Napster users who allegedly offered copies of its songs illegally on the Internet, demanding they be blocked from the service. [7] Dr. Dre was to deliver a similar list of over 200,000. [8] Some recording artists and a number of production organizations have supported the plaintiffs. [9] Napster's supporters include recording artists, some of which receive sponsorship from the company. [10] Representatives of both sides of the controversy testified before the Senate Judiciary Committee. [11] It decided to stay out of the dispute, which has drawn heavy lobbying in Washington. [12] In August, a judge ordered Napster to stop permitting the exchange of copyrighted music, effectively shutting the service down. [13] Despite Napster's claims of protection by the Digital Millennium Copyright Act and the Audio Home Recording Act, the judge believed it to be responsible for egregious copyright infringement. [14] On appeal, Napster was granted a stay of the order. [15] The recording industry's appeal of the stay will be heard in October.
