[1] In December 1997, 122 countries signed the Ottawa Treaty for the elimination of anti-personnel land mines. [2] By September 2000, the number rose to 138. [3] The treaty went into effect in March 1999, six months after the required minimum 40 countries ratified it in record time. [4] Signatories include Canada, Britain, Italy, Spain, Austria, Norway, Cambodia, Jordan, Guinea-Bissau, South Africa, Namibia, Angola, Senegal, Sudan, Tanzania, Zambia, and the seven members of Portuguese-speaking States. [5] Some countries, including Tanzania and Zambia, signed but haven't ratified the treaty. [6] Signatories Angola, Cambodia, Senegal and Sudan haven't ratified it, and continue to lay mines. [7] Countries which have ratified the treaty include Namibia, France, Germany, Britain, Burkina Faso, and Thailand. [8] Kenya will ratify it in 2000. [9] By September 2000, 101 ratified the treaty. [10] A number refuse to sign. [11] Lebanon won't unless Israel does. [12] Israel refuses, citing defense reasons. [13] Ukraine can't afford the cost of required mine destruction. [14] The US cites the need to protect South Korea from possible North Korean invasion. [15] China and Russian refuse for defense reasons. [16] Other non-signatories include Yugoslavia, Albania, Democratic Republic of the Congo, most Middle East nations and many Asian nations. [17] In the treaty's first year, some 10 million stockpiled mines were destroyed, and trade and shipments were halted. [18] However, many countries still refuse to adopt the treaty. [19] It has no governing organization to oversee it and no way to punish violators. [20] Anti-personnel land mines continue to be used in conflicts, produced and stockpiled, often by countries which have signed the treaty.
