[1] In 1996, Energy Resources Australia (ERA) requested approval for the Jabiluka uranium mine on the edge of the Kakadu National Park in the Northern Territory. [2] ERA's second uranium mine in the area would generate billions of US dollars in revenue and create 2,000 jobs. [3] Opposition parties, conservationists and local Mirrar Aboriginal people opposed the mine despite ERA assurances that it wouldn't adversely affect the area. [4] After a protracted legal battle and amid continuing protests, ERA received approval and began mine construction in June 1998. [5] Police reported hundreds of arrests as protesters blockaded the site, firebombed an ERA office, and infiltrated an ERA general meeting. [6] Two aboriginal protestors won the 1999 Goldman Environmental Prize for having delayed the mine project. [7] A UNESCO World Heritage Bureau delegation visited the area in October 1998 and concluded the Jabiluka mine would pose a severe danger to the cultural and natural values of the adjacent Kakadu National Park. [8] The report recommended the Kakadu World Heritage Area be declared endangered. [9] Australia's Environmental Minister denounced the report as biased and unsubstantiated. [10] The bureau gave the government until April 15, 1999 to provide a comprehensive rebuttal. [11] Meanwhile, the government allowed work to continue, despite UNESCO's recommendation it be halted. [12] Australia's rebuttal cited errors of science, law and logic, and declared UNESCO's findings inconsistent with the fact that another nearby mine has operated for 20 years without adverse impact. [13] While the conservative government continued to support the mine, opposition parties vowed to halt operations should they win upcoming elections.
