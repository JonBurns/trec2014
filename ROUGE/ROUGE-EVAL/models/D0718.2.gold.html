[2] By 1999 Starbucks had joint ventures with United Airlines, Barnes & Nobel and Starwood hotels. [3] Frappaccino was delivered through the Pepsi Cola's distribution network. [4] Kraft Foods sold Starbucks' coffee beans. [5] After failing to acquire Williams-Sonoma, Starbucks partnered with Oxygen Media to develop a lifestyle-oriented web portal and cable television business targeting upscale women and a web site selling gourmet food, kitchen products, home furnishings and Starbucks-branded coffee products and music. [6] Mail-order wine retailer Geerlings & Wade and www.cooking.com became Starbucks partners. [7] In 2000 online delivery service Kozmo.com placed boxes in Starbucks stores for return of videos, games, DVDs -- Kozmo would sell Starbucks products online. [8] The New York Times newspaper sold in Starbucks stores and The Times advertised Starbucks. [9] In 1998 Starbucks acquired Seattle Coffee Company in the U.K. [10] Starbucks acquired Pasqua Coffee's 56 U.S. outlets. [11] In 1999 Starbucks acquired the Tazo tea company. [12] Starbucks acquired Hear Music to produce all Starbucks CDs. [13] In 1999 Starbucks expanded from 1,688 to 2336 stores in 32 states, the District of Columbia, four Canadian provinces and the U.K. [14] Starbucks planned to open 500 stores overseas by 2003, mainly adding 400 to its 100 shops already in the Pacific Rim. [15] In 1999 a new Starbucks subsidiary handled specialty ice cream, bottled coffee drinks, and other products sold outside Starbucks shops. [16] Starbucks diversified by introducing a line of premium chocolates and "Joe", a magazine published jointly with Time, Inc.
