[1] High unemployment is France's main economic problem, despite recent improvements. [2] A top worry of French people, it is a factor affecting France's high suicide rate. [3] Long-term unemployment causes social exclusion and threatens France's social cohesion. [4] Joblessness among young people contributes rising crime and violence. [5] High unemployment sparked numerous demonstrations over the years. [6] Hundreds protested G-7 policies during the 1996 summit. [7] In December 1997 and 1998, French unions and jobless workers protested, demanding bonuses of 3,000-5,000 francs and increased benefits. [8] Rising unemployment in 1996 was partly attributed to the summer season, when graduating youths enter the job market, and France's stagnant economic growth. [9] The Socialist Party blamed failed government policies. [10] OECD's 1997 report found France's labor costs for low-skilled workers too high, and recommended a more frugal benefit system and minimum wage reform. [11] Britain blamed France's over-regulation. [12] In response to the problem, France raised unemployment benefits by 2.45% in 1996 and enacted a law encouraging businesses to cut working hours and hire more workers. [13] In 1997, when unemployment peaked at 12.6%, the government planned to spend 15-20 billion francs on employment programs. [14] The Communist Party proposed a 1,500 francs subsidy for each unemployed worker over age 55. [15] In 1998, the government announced emergency measures to support jobless people, including an additional 500 million franc unemployment aid package. [16] The Socialist Prime Minister allocated one billion francs to help the needy, and decided to raise welfare allowances by 3%, an amount protesters thought too small. [17] Unemployment has gradually declined to 10.6% in 2000.
