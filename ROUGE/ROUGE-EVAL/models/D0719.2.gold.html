[1] France had a history of keeping unemployment low in the 1960s and early 1970s. [2] In 1975, joblessness rose to 4.2 percent and in 1995 it reached 11.5 percent. [3] By mid-1996 the joblessness rate was 12.5 percent or over three million unemployed people. [4] Nearly 37 percent of the unemployed had been jobless for more than a year. [5] A French poll in 1996 identified unemployment as the most important issue facing France. [6] France had the fourth highest suicide rate in Europe. [7] This high rate was attributed to the economic crisis and unemployment. [8] France also experienced a rise in crime and violence among unemployed young people in working-class suburbs. [9] During the 1998 and 1999 Christmas seasons there were protests in Paris and other cities against unemployment. [10] Protesters demanded increases in unemployment benefits and Christmas bonuses for unemployed workers. [11] Critics attributed France's unemployment to its near-zero growth economic environment, to its focus on fairness in income distribution, to rigidity in budget policy, to declines in social policy, and to layoffs in the public sector. [12] Additional unemployment causes cited were high costs for low-skilled workers, high unemployment insurance and compensation costs, and over-regulation of industry. [13] Critics suggested using the money paying for social aid programs to be used instead for investment in jobs. [14] To support the jobless, the government increased spending on unemployment aid and on funding for employment programs. [15] Despite ongoing reforms, unemployment stood at about 12 percent in 1997. [16] The unemployment situation, however, improved steadily from mid-1997 to the end of the decade.
