[1] Unemployment is one of France's worst economic problems. [2] In the 1990s unemployment increased considerably and remained high until July 1997 when the Socialist Government came into power. [3] At that time the rate began to drop slowly. [4] By December 1999 the unemployment rate had dropped to the lowest level in seven years. [5] High unemployment contributed to a rise in crime and violence, particularly among young people. [6] Long-time unemployment resulted in social exclusion that threatened the French social cohesion. [7] Many people on unemployment for a long duration found themselves impoverished. [8] High unemployment was also a major cause of increased suicides. [9] The Socialist Party blamed government policy as a major factor behind rising unemployment. [10] Others felt unemployment increases were a consequence of the G-7 policies that militate against the well being of the majority. [11] Tony Blair blamed over-regulation for high employment. [12] An OECD report blamed the rise on substantial structural impediments in the functioning of labor markets in France and recommended a more flexible public sector management. [13] The government took several steps to help mitigate the situation. [14] France raised its unemployment benefits in July 1994 and 1995 and subsidized employment programs. [15] They enacted a law that encouraged businesses to cut working hours and hire more workers. [16] In January 1998 they announced several emergency measures to support jobless people and promised to continue communications with the unions and anti-unemployment associations on terms to be adopted by the government in favor of jobless people.
