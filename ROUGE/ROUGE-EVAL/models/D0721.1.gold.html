[1] On October 12, 1998 gay Wyoming University student Matthew Shepard, 21, died after being kidnapped, pistol-whipped and tied to a fence in near-freezing temperatures outside Laramie, by Russell Henderson, 21, and Aaron McKinney, 22. [2] On October 19 hundreds were arrested as 5,000 protested in New York City. [3] At trial in March 1999, Henderson pleaded guilty to kidnap and murder for two consecutive life sentences, without parole. [4] Accomplice after-the-fact, Chasity Pasley, faced sentencing. [5] Accomplice after-the-fact, Kristen Price, faced trial. [6] Anti-homosexual bias legislation died in Wyoming and other mountain states. [7] In June 1999, California's Assembly added disability, gender and sexual orientation to hate crime legislation, to extend the maximum sentence to life. [8] More than 450 bills were introduced nation-wide on gay and lesbian issues. [9] At trial in October, McKinney's attorney pleaded that McKinney committed manslaughter because of "gay panic" to homosexual advances from Shepard, because McKinney had been abused homosexually at age 7 and had consensual homosexual sex at 15. [10] The prosecution sought the death penalty arguing McKinney fully intended to murder Shepard after kidnapping and robbing him. [11] In November 1999 McKinney was barred from using a "gay panic" defense, which begs "temporary insanity" or "diminished capacity" not within Wyoming law. [12] McKinney received two consecutive life sentences for murder, kidnapping and aggravated robbery. [13] In July Congress did not pass legislation outlawing anti-homosexual bias crimes. [14] In June 2000 a new Senate amendment would add sexual orientation, sex or disability to 1968 federal hate crimes law, while exercising federally protected activities, such as voting.
