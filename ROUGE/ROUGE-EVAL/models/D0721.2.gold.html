[1] In October 1998, Matthew Shepard, a gay, 21-year old University of Wyoming college student was savagely beaten and tied to a fence in near-freezing temperatures. [2] He died five days later. [3] Matthew was lured out of a bar and attacked by two high-school dropouts, Aaron McKinney, 22 and Russell Henderson, 21. [4] Henderson pleaded guilty and was sentenced to two consecutive life terms. [5] McKinney went to trial, was found guilty and sentenced to two consecutive life terms. [6] The killing galvanized gays and lesbians nationwide. [7] Over 5,000 marched in Manhattan. [8] Candlelight vigils and campus rallies took place across the country. [9] Over 450 bills on issues important to gays and lesbians were introduced in legislatures across the country in 1999, prompted in part by the murder of Matthew Shepard. [10] Spokesmen for the gay community lashed out at right-wing organizations such as the Family Research Council and criticized public figures such as then Senate Majority Leader Trent Lott for saying that homosexuality is a sin and gay people should be helped just like alcoholics or kleptomaniacs. [11] A New York Times writer said "Once any group is successfully scapegoated as a subhuman threat to "normal" values by a propaganda machine, emboldened thugs take over. [12] Shepard's father urged Congress to adopt legislation that would extend federal hate-crime protection to gays and lesbians. [13] The U.S. Senate approved such legislation, but the House did not. [14] In 2000, "The Laramie Project," a collaboration between a New York-based theater troupe and the people of Laramie, Wyoming, was well received.
