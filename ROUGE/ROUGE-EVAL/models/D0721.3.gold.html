[1] Matthew Shepard, a gay University of Wyoming student, died after being robbed, beaten, and left to die on a fence outside of Laramie, Wyoming. [2] His murder galvanized national outrage over anti-homosexual violence. [3] There were candlelight vigils across the country and a march in New York. [4] Religious right leaders, while deploring violence towards gays, insisted they hated the sin, not the sinner. [5] Two suspects, Aaron McKinney and Russell Henderson, were charged with first-degree murder. [6] Henderson pleaded guilty to avoid the death penalty and was sentenced to two life terms. [7] McKinney went to trial in October 1998, close to the one-year anniversary of Shepard's death. [8] Prosecutor Cal Rerucha claimed that McKinney and Russell Henderson intended to murder Shepard after kidnapping and robbing him. [9] Jason Tangeman, one of McKinney's defense lawyers, conceded McKinney was responsible for Shepard's death. [10] In attempting to get his client a life sentence rather than the death penalty, Tangeman argued that McKinney's past, which included sexual abuse as a child, caused him to lash out in rage at Shepard's sexual advances. [11] The presiding judge, Barton Voight, barred McKinney's attorneys from using a "gay panic" defense. [12] The jury found McKinney guilty of murder and he was sentenced to life imprisonment in a deal approved by Shepard's parents. [13] Shepard's father appeared in a press conference in front of the US Capital urging Congress to pass legislation that would extend hate-crime protection to gays and lesbians. [14] Twenty-two states have hate-crime laws that include sexual orientation while 20 have laws that do not.
