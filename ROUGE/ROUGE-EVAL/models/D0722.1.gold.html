[1] In 1996 the Pentagon took $2 billion from space-based Ballistic Missile Defense, shifting to ground-based next generation PAC-3 Patriot systems and "Navy Air Defense" anti-missile systems. [2] President Clinton vetoed a ground-based $60 billion National Missile Defense system, concerned about violating the 1972 anti-ballistic missile treaty with Russia, and because rogue nation long-range missiles couldn't yet reach the US. [3] In 1997 NMD tests successfully detected and tracked incoming long-range missiles. [4] By 1998 Theater High-Altitude Area Defense systems had failed 5 of 8 flight tests. [5] By 1999 THAAD had failed 9 of 13 flight tests, due to deficient target-seeking systems on interceptors. [6] Clinton committed NMD deployment, after North Korea tested missiles over Japan. [7] An interceptor destroyed a separated warhead in space. [8] THAAD skipped final tests and entered "initial low-rate production", still facing flight tests before fielding in 2007. [9] Critics called the tests unrealistic and easily fooled by simple countermeasures. [10] Alaska got the first of two THAAD sites. [11] Russia and the US reopened ABM Treaty talks. [12] In 2000 one of two interceptor tests failed, delaying NMD approval. [13] Russia threatened withdrawal from all strategic and conventional treaties, if the US withdrew from the ABM Treaty, exposing Britain with no ABM protection. [14] Then China might modernize its nuclear weapons, India respond, Pakistan follow, and Japan go nuclear. [15] Mutual Assured Destruction might better deter rogue nation missile attacks. [16] A third interceptor test failed. [17] Iran tested a long-range missile. [18] THAAD ground radar distinguished a warhead from decoys. [19] A ground-to-interceptor link test succeeded. [20] Clinton deferred NMD approval indefinitely.
