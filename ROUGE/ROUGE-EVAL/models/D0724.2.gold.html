[1] "Obesity is the No. 1 malnutrition problem in the US". [2] By 1999 more than half of US "citizens" were chronically overweight (body mass index 25) and 22% were obese (BMI 30 or more), up from 12.5% in 1991. [3] The number of obese people more than tripled from 1991 to 90 million in 1999. [4] The number of obese children doubled in 20 years. [5] The entire US population could be overweight in a few generations. [6] Forty-five states have obesity rates over 15%. [7] In the southeast, obesity rates jumped 67.2% in 5 years, reflecting more sedentary lifestyles than nationally. [8] Georgia lead with a 101.8 percent increase. [9] Approximately 300,000 deaths a year are attributed to this obesity epidemic. [10] US obesity is due to sedentary lifestyles and readily-available "super-sized" calorie-and-fat rich foods. [11] Although US fat intake dropped the past 20 years from 40% to 33% of calories consumed, average daily calorie intake rose from 1,854 to 2.002, translating to 15 pounds gained per year. [12] Commercial food portions tempt over-consumption and establish too much food as "normal". [13] Physical activity rates have not changed significantly in decades. [14] Populations gain weight as they abandon traditional lower-calorie diets for prosperous modern diets with more calories. [15] Consensus disease models explain obesity causes and relate obesity to major causes of death, such as heart disease, diabetes and hypertension. [16] Fat cells are endocrine organs that produce leptin, which controls appetite. [17] Genetic abnormalities don't explain wide-spread obesity, but do offer insights into genetic, drug and diet interventions for obesity cases incurable by diet.
