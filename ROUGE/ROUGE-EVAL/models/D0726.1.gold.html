[1] Al Gore formed a campaign organization at the start of 1999. [2] The only obvious opponent for the Democratic presidential nomination in 2000 was Sen. Bill Bradley, D-N.J. [3] Gore began touring the country gathering support from diverse groups and talking to citizens one-on-one to overcome his reputation for stiffness. [4] He appointed women and blacks to his campaign staff to counter his reputation of operating with an inner circle of white males. [5] He gained an early endorsement from Minority Leader Gephardt and polls in early 1999 showed him a clear favorite for the Democratic nomination, but a distinct underdog when pitted against potential Republican candidate George W. Bush. [6] By the end of April the campaign had raised $8.9 million. [7] During the remainder of 1999 Gore received endorsements from many prestigious people including Hillary Clinton, the Governor and both California Senators, 600 Latino leaders, and Rep. Patrick Kennedy. [8] But polls indicated a stronger than expected challenge by Bill Bradley. [9] The lingering effects of the Clinton impeachment trial posed a problem which Gore hoped to palliate by moving campaign headquarters from lobbyists' row on K Street in Washington back to the folks at home in Gore's native state of Tennessee in October. [10] Sen. Ted Kennedy endorsed Gore at the start of 2000 and assisted with campaigning for the New Hampshire primary. [11] By the end of February Gore was ahead of Bradley in polls 56%-32%. [12] By August Gore and Sen. Lieberman made up the Democratic presidential ticket.
