[1] The antiviral drug interferon is a genetically engineered replica of a naturally occurring human protein. [2] A weak angiogenesis inhibitor, interferon blocks a substance secreted by some tumors that signals the body to grow more cells. [3] It has been used most prominently either alone or in combination with ribavirin to treat liver disease, particularly hepatitis B and hepatitis C, but has also been effective in stopping the growth of giant-cell tumors. [4] Its effectiveness in reducing inflammation and quieting down overactive immune systems has helped delay the onset and slow down the progress of multiple sclerosis. [5] Used most extensively in the treatment of hepatitis C, interferon typically reduces symptoms of about 20% of patients if administered for six months and achieves a substantial response in as many as 30% if continued for 12-18 months. [6] It eliminates the disease in only 10-20% of patients and about half of those treated do not respond at all. [7] On the positive side, it has saved the lives of many children with life-threatening tumors called hemangiomas for which no other cancer treatment had worked. [8] Treatment with interferon often results in physical and emotional side effects. [9] Patients complain that flu symptoms, lethargy, clogged saliva glands, chronic yeast infection, urinary infection and depression can often make the treatment almost as devastating as the disease.
