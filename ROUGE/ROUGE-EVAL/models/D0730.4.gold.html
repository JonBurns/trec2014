[1] In the words of President Clinton the line item veto "is very important in helping to preserve the integrity of federal spending". [2] The line item veto has been sought by presidents since Grant and was popularized by Reagan. [3] It was part of Republican "Contract with America" led by Speaker Newt Gingrich that enacted it. [4] The line item veto allows the president to veto particular items in spending bills and certain limited tax provisions passed by Congress. [5] Previously the president could only veto entire bills. [6] Bill Clinton is the only president to have had line item veto authority. [7] He has said that it should be used sparingly. [8] He used it 163 times, mostly to delete items from the military construction bill. [9] The line item veto was challenged by a group of most Democratic senators but was dismissed by the Supreme Court. [10] However, another challenge led by New York Mayor Giuliani and Idaho farmers resulted in a federal judge declaring the line item veto unconstitutional. [11] The Justice Department appealed that decision to the Supreme Court. [12] The Supreme Court rejected the line item veto as a departure from the basic constitutional requirement that presidents accept or reject bills in their entirety. [13] The Court found that the line item veto violates the "presentment clause" of Article I that establishes the process by which a bill becomes law. [14] The Court vote was 6-3 with Justice Stevens writing for the majority.
