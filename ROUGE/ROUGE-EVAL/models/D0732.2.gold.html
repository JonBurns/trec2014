[1] Between early 1996 to late 2000, the Kenyan government initiated a number of programs to improve the educational level of its population. [2] The emphasis is on raising enrollment levels, increasing the learning of science subjects, furnishing conservation studios, emphasizing health issues, and making improvements in business and industrial knowledge. [3] Phase Two of a plan to increase enrollment rates in primary schools developed by the Kenyan and British governments is scheduled with approximately $30 million provided by the Overseas Development Authority. [4] It is aimed at training primary school head teachers. [5] Between 1990 and 1995, 23 percent of students dropped out before reaching grade five as a result of lack of adequate teachers and teaching facilities in many schools. [6] The Kenyan Minister for Education announced that his ministry would soon increase the salary of secondary school science subject teachers to attract more teachers to the career. [7] The government is providing family life education, issuing new guidelines to liberalize the education sector, and instituting a sex education program. [8] It has revised previous rulings to create a more conducive environment for the private sector to be involved in higher education. [9] An early childhood development program of $34 million for over one million children and parents has been implemented. [10] Kenya is also strengthening education and training programs for the disabled and for street children as well as giving second chances to dropouts. [11] It has issued new guidelines to procure textbooks and training equipment and is working with women's groups to improve women's status.
