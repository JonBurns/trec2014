[1] In 1996, the Kenyan Minister of Education appealed to students to study science and announced that the salaries of science teachers would be increased. [2] The President ordered all schools to provide education on environmental conservation. [3] Kenya started an early childhood program for pre-school children and their parents. [4] The government launched the Strengthening Primary Education Project, designed to train primary school teachers, improve children's health, increase enrollment in primary schools, and purchase textbooks and science equipment. [5] The government planned to strengthen family life education for children in order to teach them about health problems including HIV/AIDS. [6] There was a decision to introduce enterprise education in primary schools to create a business culture among children. [7] In 1998 the World Bank gave a grant to Kenya to help develop early childhood education because of a need for well-trained pre-school teachers. [8] In 1999 a 4-day education expo was held for exhibitors from across Kenya, as well as from France and Australia. [9] Despite substantial increases in allocations, the student drop-out rate increased. [10] The President challenged investors to consider investing in higher education in Kenya. [11] In 2000 Kenya planned an unspecified new school program to replace its traditional education system. [12] The U.S. was to give $350,000 to a project to link Kenya's schools to the internet. [13] In September Kenya launched a sex education program, the product of joint efforts by local and international donors through the International AIDS Control Council. [14] The government passed a bill to allow foreign universities to establish constituent campuses in Kenya.
