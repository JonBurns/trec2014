[1] In 1998 the US Department of Interior announced it would remove 2,500 wolves in Minnesota, Michigan and Wisconsin from the Endangered Species Act. [2] Smaller populations in Montana, Wyoming and Idaho would be reclassified from "endangered" to "threatened". [3] The new policies would not diminish protection of wolves in Yellowstone national Park and central Idaho. [4] Nor would they change the status of endangered Mexican gray wolves being reintroduced in the Southwest, or red wolves in the Southeast. [5] Many early reintroduction projects were thwarted by ranchers killing wolves ostensibly to protect their animals, despite federal livestock compensation, prison penalties, $100,000 fines, and $30,000 rewards. [6] Unrescinded state wolf bounties also undermined reintroduction programs. [7] Reintroductions were more successful at remote sites, with more law enforcement officers in the area and better outreach to residents, visitors and hunter organizations. [8] By 1999 gray wolves were rebounding in the Northern Rockies and upper Great Lakes, allowing protections to lift. [9] More than 30 packs of 400-500 wolves inhabited Wyoming, Montana and Idaho. [10] Some 2,500 wolves were in Minnesota, Wisconsin and Michigan. [11] Mexican wolves were coming back in the Southwest with difficulty. [12] Red wolves were reestablished in the Southeast. [13] The number of continental US wolves doubled and their range tripled. [14] Timber wolves in Canada were unlikely to recolonize the northeastern US on their own due to habitat discontinuities and legal killing, forcing deliberate reintroduction. [15] Federal reintroduction programs overcame state legal challenges. [16] On the verge of extinction 40 years ago in may countries; wolves have recovered world-wide due to new protections.
