[1] Mining operations in central and South America generally showed increased productivity. [2] Copper, gold, diamonds, iron, nickel, silver, zinc, tin and bauxite were among the elements and ores involved with gold receiving the most attention. [3] The increase came about from several factors including resurgence in the gold industry and more investment from domestic and foreign sources. [4] Peru's resurgent gold mining operations, with some Thai investment, moved from the number 12 producer to number 7 during the 1996-2000 period. [5] Peru also produces copper, zinc, silver, tin and iron. [6] Gold mining in Mexico increased, putting that country to number 17 in the world. [7] Chile dominates the world in copper production and has received some foreign investment for gold and silver mining. [8] Gold mining initiatives are going forward in Venezuela and it will soon have the second largest diamond mine in South America. [9] South African mining companies are operating in Venezuela and Peru. [10] Brazil has increased it diamond production and is seventh in the world. [11] Canadian interests have invested in gold mining in Costa Rica. [12] Argentina has placed in the top 20 in gold production. [13] Environmentalists have objected deforestation in Venezuela in order to erect electrical power lines in support of gold mining. [14] The work on this project has also destroyed many agricultural crops. [15] The groups most concerned have organized protests. [16] Latin America's largest gold mine, the Yanacocha mine in Peru was responsible for a mercury spill that poisoned dozens of Andean villagers.
