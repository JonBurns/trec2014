[1] Brazil is South America's leading gold producer, while its diamond production ranks seventh in the world. [2] Peru's chief export is copper (in addition to silver, tin, and iron), yet it's gold industry, second only to Brazil's, began resurgence in the mid-90s buoyed by foreign investment. [3] In 1996, an especially rich seam of ore was discovered that led to the country becoming the world's eighth largest gold manufacture in 1998. [4] In 2000, it was the seventh, with the industry expecting continued growth due to long-term investments. [5] In August of 2000, the Peruvian government fined South America's largest goldmine $500,000 for a mercury spill that poisoned dozens of Andean villagers. [6] Chile leads the world in copper production. [7] In 1996, private and state-run mines produced a combined total of 4.7 million metric tons of refined copper with additional mining projects going into operation that year. [8] By 2000, Chile's copper, gold, and silver production reached an all-time high. [9] In Venezuela, where gold reserves represent approximately 10% of the world's total, future production rates are expected to grow rapidly thanks to massive investment. [10] In 1998, Indians near Caracas initiated a protest against planned mining operations in their territory; they'd been promised previously that the area would not be mined for gold or diamonds. [11] A diamond mine began operations in the Venezuelan state of Bolivar in 2001, with expectations of becoming South America's second largest diamond mine. [12] Argentina was expected to be among the world's top 20 gold producers by 2000, according to government projections.
