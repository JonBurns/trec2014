[1] Venezuela produces gold, coal, iron, nickel, and bauxite. [2] Its gold reserves are 10 percent of the world's total, with Latin America's richest deposit in the Imataca rain forest. [3] A 20-percent income tax cut encouraged investment. [4] Gold and diamond mining was legalized in the Imataca in 1997 and international companies replaced local miners. [5] In 1998 Pemon Indians blocked Venezuela's International Highway to protest a high-voltage power line being built through the rain forest and in 1999 protested President Chavez's failure to halt mining. [6] The world's largest diamond-bearing kimberlita was found in Toco and mining was to start in 2001. [7] Peru's gold production grew from the world's 12th biggest in 1996 to 7th in 2000. [8] Copper mining is also important, followed by zinc, lead, refined silver, tin, and iron. [9] Peru held gold symposiums in 1994 and 1996 to promote international investment. [10] A newly discovered seam made Peru's Yanacocha gold mine the biggest in South America. [11] In 2000 the government fined the Yanacocha for a mercury spill. [12] Chile is the world's leading copper producer, producing 4.7 million metric tons of refined copper in 1996. [13] It also produces gold, silver, and nitrates. [14] Mining investment reached $2.4 billion in 1998. [15] Brazil diamond production ranks 7th in the world. [16] Increased investment in Mexican gold mining put it in 17th place worldwide in 1995. [17] Argentina is among the world's top 20 gold producers. [18] Central American countries and Canada signed a 1997 agreement on environmental rules for the mining industry. [19] Venezuela and South Africa opened reciprocal embassies.
