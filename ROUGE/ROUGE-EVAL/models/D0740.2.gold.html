[1] On March 21, 1999, after 19 days, 21 hours and 55 minutes aloft, the first persons to circumnavigate the globe in a hot-air balloon touched down in Egypt's Sahara Desert. [2] Swiss psychiatrist Bertrand Piccard and British adventurer Brian Jones had traveled non-stop for 29,206 miles to complete their record-setting journey. [3] Piccard had made two previous attempts at the feat in 1997 and 1998 with two differing teams--the first was abandoned after only six hours aloft because of a hazardous fuel leak; the second attempt was aborted after the balloon was denied permission to fly through China's airspace. [4] On this third, successful voyage, the team launched from Switzerland and drifted southwestward to North Africa before catching a favorable jet stream at 9.27Â° west longitude. [5] From there the team flew east across Asia, over the Pacific for six days to Mexico, then across the Atlantic to complete the final leg of their journey. [6] Piccard and Jones piloted their innovative, helium/hot-air hybrid balloon-- the Breitling Orbiter 3--by controlling the craft's rising and falling in order to catch the winds (typically jet streams) that seemed to be going in the most promising direction; satellite communication, navigation, and weather monitoring systems kept them in touch with their control centers, and provided guidance essential to success and survival. [7] Achieving what was hailed as the last great milestone of aviation, the two received awards from the International Olympic Committee and the Federation Aeronautique Internationale, as well as a $1 million prize from Anheuser-Bush Co.
