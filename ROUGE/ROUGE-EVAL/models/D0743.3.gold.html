[1] Turkey's August 1999 earthquakes with 7.4 magnitude and 1,300 aftershocks were its largest since 1976 and first in Turkey's densely populated industrial west. [2] Final death estimates were 15,000-17,000. [3] Over 100,000 buildings and the Golcuk naval base were destroyed. [4] Bridges, highways, power stations, and water and telephone lines were damaged. [5] A fire at Turkey's largest oil refinery was finally extinguished but produced widespread pollution. [6] 200,000 were made homeless and thousands lived outdoors unsheltered from heavy rains. [7] Disease was feared. [8] Turkish rescue efforts were plagued by inexperience, poor organization and lack of supplies. [9] Countries worldwide sent money, equipment, expertise, relief workers, and shelters. [10] NGOs jumped in to help. [11] Factories established tent camps for their displaced workers. [12] The area hit provided almost half Turkey's tax revenue. [13] Economic damage was estimated at $20-40 billion. [14] The government lacked cash for infrastructure repair and threatened to relapse into recession. [15] Turkey expected loans and grants for rebuilding. [16] Lax building codes and unscrupulous contractors and politicians contributed to the destruction by allowing substandard buildings on fault lines. [17] Cheap cement buildings collapsed. [18] Turkey prepared extensively for new quakes, including building coordination headquarters in fields, giving officials walkie-talkies, and requiring inspections and insurance for new buildings. [19] Those whose substandard buildings collapsed and caused fatalities were arrested. [20] A year later 150,000 were in prefab homes and only 26,000 still in tent cities. [21] Criticism dissipated with government-subsidized rents and free meals. [22] Response to subsequent smaller quakes was efficient. [23] Booming construction helped the economy. [24] The EU made Turkey a member-candidate. [25] Psychological damage remained.
