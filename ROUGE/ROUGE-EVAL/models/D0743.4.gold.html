[1] A massive earthquake measuring 7.4 on the Richter scale hit western Turkey at 3:00 am local time on August 17, 1999. [2] The epicenter was near the industrial city of Izmit, about 70 kilometers southeast of Istanbul. [3] Relief workers, money, and rescue equipment poured in from around the world. [4] The casualties included more than 12,000 dead, up to 35,000 missing, and thousands injured. [5] The destruction was tremendous, with water pipes broken, electric lines down, and roads impassible. [6] An oil refinery fire was finally under control by August 21 with the help of foreign rescue workers, but 9 of the 30 oil refinery tanks were damaged or destroyed. [7] In the ensuing weeks there were 1300 aftershocks in the region including an earthquake measuring 5.0. [8] The survivors continued to suffer as heavy rains battered the region. [9] Hospitals and field hospitals continued to be flooded with victims. [10] Early estimates of economic loss were between $20 and 40 billion. [11] The quake's enormous social and economic impact continued to grow as the government struggled for money to repair basic infrastructure. [12] About a year later another strong earthquake hit the same region. [13] Most Turks believed that the government had done a good job in the post-quake cleanup. [14] A year later about 26,000 survivors were living in tent cities that had initially held some 120,000; and some 150,00 people were still in prefabricated houses. [15] The state was providing rent subsidies as well as free meals to some survivors.
