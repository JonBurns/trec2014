[1] Organic farming practices prohibit the use of synthetic fertilizers and pesticides for crops and the use of antibiotics or hormones in livestock. [2] The organic food industry grew in the US in the 19990s at a 20 percent annual rate. [3] Sales in 2000 were expected to reach $6 billion. [4] Estimates were that the industry would grow in the 2000s about 25 percent a year. [5] Organic food products, while mainly available in specialty stores, were becoming increasingly available in mainstream stores. [6] Once a political statement, organic foods became a health statement, especially by affluent baby-boomers. [7] Critics maintained that food labeled organic was not necessarily safer or more nutritious than conventional foods. [8] Moreover, because federal standards were lacking, consumers could not be certain that food labeled organic was in fact organic; meat and poultry labeled organic could have been raised using growth hormones or antibiotics. [9] The US Department of Agriculture in 1997 proposed a national certification program for organic foods to replace a hodgepodge of state rules and industry guidelines. [10] The program, however, would have allowed foods labeled organic to contain genetically modified ingredients and to be decontaminated using irradiation. [11] It also permitted sewer sludge to be used as fertilizer. [12] More than 275,000 comments were received in response to the proposed new rules, nearly all opposed to the processes the department considered allowing. [13] The department responded in 1999 by issuing improved regulations prohibiting these processes and in 2000 by issuing a rule offering a strict standard defining what constitutes an organic food.
